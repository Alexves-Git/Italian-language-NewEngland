Key Findings · Italian Language Teaching in New England, 2020–2024
✕

Overall growth with a post-pandemic inflection. Total enrollment across New England grew from 14,013 students in 2020–21 to 16,183 in 2023–24, a net increase of +15.5% over four years. Growth was not linear: enrollment declined to 13,564 in 2021–22 — likely reflecting residual disruption from the COVID-19 pandemic — before recovering strongly in the two subsequent years.

Rhode Island leads on a population-adjusted basis. While Massachusetts accounts for the largest absolute enrollment, Rhode Island records by far the highest intensity when enrollment is normalised by population — reaching 431 students per 100,000 inhabitants in 2023–24, more than three times the Massachusetts rate of 135. This reflects longstanding Italian-heritage communities in the Providence–Cranston–Johnston corridor and a dense network of public schools with established Italian programmes. The sharp increase observed in 2023–24 also partly reflects expanded census coverage, but the underlying intensity predates that expansion.

Middle school enrolment is the primary engine of growth. The most structurally significant trend is the sharp rise in secondary I grade (middle school) enrolment, from 1,613 students in 2020–21 to 4,160 in 2023–24 — an increase of +158%. Consistently large contributors include the Wilmington, Cranston, and Medford public school systems, as well as the Eliot K-8 Innovation School in Boston. High school enrolment, by contrast, has remained essentially flat over the same period. If this cohort effect persists, it represents a meaningful future pipeline into secondary and university-level Italian.

Primary school programmes have largely disappeared from the census record. The reverse of the middle school story is the near-total collapse of primary school reporting: from 1,236 students in 2020–21 to just 196 in 2023–24, a decline of 84%. Several large Medford-area primary programmes — each enrolling over 300 students in the baseline year — ceased to appear in subsequent survey waves. Whether this reflects actual programme discontinuation or a withdrawal from census participation is not determinable from the available data, but it represents the most concerning signal in the longitudinal record. Primary-level instruction offers the greatest potential for deep language acquisition, and the loss of visibility over this segment warrants direct follow-up.

The university sector has recovered but is increasingly concentrated. University enrolment declined from 4,607 to 4,136 between 2020–21 and 2021–22, before recovering to approximately 4,967 by 2023–24. However, the distribution is highly uneven: the five largest programmes — the University of New Hampshire, Boston College, UMass Amherst, the University of Rhode Island, and Dartmouth College — consistently account for roughly half of all university enrolment. Several smaller programmes reported zero students or did not submit data by 2023–24, suggesting that the recovery is concentrated in already well-established departments while peripheral programmes contract.

New Hampshire records the steepest proportional growth. Starting from a small baseline of 490 students in 2020–21, New Hampshire reached 1,490 students by 2022–23 before settling at 1,292 in 2023–24, representing a +164% increase in enrolment intensity over the period. This growth is driven almost entirely by the university sector — principally the University of New Hampshire (Durham) and Dartmouth College (Hanover). At the county level, Grafton County (home to Dartmouth) records one of the highest per-100,000 rates in the region.

The informal learning sector shows consistent, uninterrupted growth. Enrolment in language schools, cultural associations, and adult education programmes — the "other learning contexts" sector — grew every year without exception, from 627 students in 2020–21 to 906 in 2023–24 (+44.5%). Key contributors include the Cambridge Center for Adult Education, the Vermont Italian Cultural Association, Scuola PIB, and the Pirandello Italian Language Center. This steady progression, independent of institutional budget cycles, reflects durable community-driven demand for Italian language instruction.

Maine is the only state in consistent decline. Maine's reported enrolment fell from 310 to 153 students over the four-year period — a reduction of approximately 50% — and is the only state in which the per-100,000 rate declined continuously year on year (from 22.6 to 11.0). With only four reporting institutions, all at the university or community level, and Bowdoin College reporting no students in 2023–24, the state's Italian language presence has become increasingly fragile.

About this Dashboard
✕

The Italian Consulate General in Boston conducts an annual statistical survey on the spread of Italian language teaching in K-12 schools, universities, and other learning contexts across New England — specifically in Massachusetts, Maine, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, and Vermont.

The survey is required by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and International Cooperation (MAECI) as part of its ongoing monitoring of Italian language instruction abroad.

This dashboard visualises four academic years of census data (2020–21 through 2023–24), covering enrollment, teacher counts, and geographic distribution across three sectors: K-12 schools, universities, and other learning contexts such as language schools and cultural associations.

This figure counts the total number of students enrolled in Italian language programs reported to the Italian Consulate General in Boston across three sectors:

K-12 Schools — public and private schools at primary and secondary level offering Italian.
Universities — courses and programs at degree-granting institutions.
Other contexts — language schools, cultural associations, adult education programs, and similar.

Totals reflect only institutions that submitted enrollment data. Approximately 10% of reporting institutions did not provide student counts — actual enrollment is therefore higher than shown.

Students per 100,000 Inhabitants
✕
This is a normalized intensity measure that corrects for differences in population size between states, making them directly comparable.
students enrolled ÷ population × 100,000
State-level rate uses the full state population as the denominator (US Census Bureau Vintage 2023 annual estimates, July 1, midpointed per academic year).

City-level rate uses the county population of the county in which the city is located — a better approximation of the local population served than the state total. The county name is shown in the hover tooltip.

For example: Hanover NH (home to Dartmouth College) sits in Grafton County (~90,000 people), yielding a rate of ~310 per 100k — very different from using New Hampshire's full 1.4 million population (~20/100k).
